## Summary

This presentation applied the four-intention framework — *intentio auctoris*, *operis*,
*lectoris*, and *intentio algorithmi* — to streaming music recommendation systems,
specifically to the way a major platform's "Daily Mix" feature shapes what listeners
hear and, over time, how they understand their own tastes.

The central claim: recommendation systems do not serve the listener's *intentio
lectoris* (what the listener actually wants from the listening experience). They serve
a commercially optimized proxy for it — a model of the listener built from behavioral
data — while simultaneously shaping the *intentio auctoris* of artists who learn to
produce content that the algorithm surfaces. The result is a communication circuit in
which the algorithm is not a neutral conduit but an active fourth participant,
redirecting the intentions of both authors and audiences toward its own ends.

The professional recommendation: communicators working in digital content environments
should audit their content for algorithmic audience assumptions — not just "who is my
reader" but "what does the system that routes this content to my reader want from it,
and is that compatible with what I am trying to say?"

**Reflection** *(added for portfolio)*

The hardest part of this presentation was the distinction between *intentio lectoris*
and the algorithmic model of *intentio lectoris*. Hall gives you the tools to see that
preferred readings are constructed — but he is writing about television producers and
audiences, not about a system that constructs a model of the audience from behavioral
traces and then routes content to match that model. Van Dijck's platform logic is what
closes the gap: the algorithm is not reading the room, it is engineering the room.
